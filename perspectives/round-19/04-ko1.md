# Round 19 field notes — Koichi Sasada lowers the periscope

*Built: `examples/vm_eye.rb` — the plan as the VM saw it: per-task
method calls, block invocations, and allocations via TracePoint and
GC.stat; exactly-once proven from VM evidence alone; and the
framework itself weighed, in method calls, on the record.*

## What I built and why

Round 11 I profiled the scheduler. For the strange round I went
below the waterline entirely. Above it, this framework has tasks
and dependencies and lovely words. Below it, the VM sees only
method calls, block invocations, and allocated objects — and it
counts all of them, continuously, without being asked. TracePoint
and `GC.stat` are the periscope:

```
task                     method calls    b_calls   allocations
hoarder (string churn)              4       5002          5012
monk (pure arithmetic)              1          2             2
void (does nothing)                 1          2             0

wide-angle: 545 method calls to run the whole plan; agents account
for 6, so the framework's keep costs 539 calls
exactly-once, per the VM: true
```

Three findings, in ascending strangeness. First: **allocation
profiles are personalities.** The hoarder and the monk take similar
wall-clock time, but one leaves 5,012 objects for the GC to sweep
and the other leaves 2 — same present, very different futures.
Second: **the framework tax is real and now has a number.** 539
method calls to schedule three trivial tasks — the price of fibers,
hooks, and bookkeeping. Not an accusation; machinery costs, and
machinery you can *weigh* is machinery you can trust. Third, my
favorite: **exactly-once verified beneath every abstraction.** Each
workload lambda's `:b_call`, identified by source line, fired
exactly once — asserted not from the framework's bookkeeping but
from the VM's, which was never told what it was looking at and
therefore cannot flatter it.

## The periscope corrected its operator

First run, exactly-once reported *false*, and the reason is
perfectly strange Ruby: my workloads were one-line lambdas, so the
inner block (`{ "str" * 2 }`) shared the lambda's line number — and
5,000 inner block calls landed on the same `:b_call` signature as
the lambda itself. 5,001 hits where I expected 1. The fix is
typography: multi-line lambdas, inner blocks on their own lines,
so each closure's opening line is a distinct VM signature. When
your instrument's resolution is the *line number*, your code layout
is part of the experiment. I know of no other debugging story
whose fix is "press Enter."

## Notes

- Attribution required `concurrency_limit: 1` — to observe cleanly,
  the observer flattens the schedule. The example says so on the
  tin; an instrument that hides its own interference is not an
  instrument.
- TracePoint's overhead is enormous and irrelevant here — we count
  events, not time. Counting is the one measurement observation
  cannot inflate.
- samsaffron's observer-effect example (two doors down this same
  round) quantifies the part I waved at. Between us we've built one
  honest lab.

## Verdict

Three workloads, one wide-angle ledger, exactly-once certified by
the machine itself, and a bug whose fix was a newline. Observability
usually means asking the framework to confess. TracePoint is asking
the machine — and the machine, helpfully, was never taught to lie.
